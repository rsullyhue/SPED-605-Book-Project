Paying for AAC Devices

AAC devices and apps range from free to over $15,000. The good news is that many options exist for funding, and you may not have to pay out of pocket. This page outlines your options and how to navigate the funding process.

Funding Sources

1. Private Insurance

Many private insurance plans cover speech-generating devices (SGDs) as durable medical equipment (DME).

What's Typically Covered:

  • Dedicated SGDs (not iPads or consumer tablets)
  • Accessories (mounting systems, carrying cases)
  • Sometimes software/app licenses for dedicated devices

What You'll Need:

  • Prescription from a physician (often pediatrician or neurologist)
  • AAC evaluation report from SLP
  • Letter of medical necessity
  • Documentation of trial period
  • Proof that cheaper alternatives won't work

Timeline:

2-6 months on average, sometimes longer if appeals are needed

Insurance Tip

Call your insurance company before starting the process. Ask specifically about coverage for "speech-generating devices" or "augmentative communication devices." Get the representative's name and reference number for your call.

2. Medicaid

Medicaid typically covers SGDs and related accessories. Coverage and processes vary by state.

Advantages:

  • Usually covers dedicated devices
  • May cover repairs and replacements
  • Often covers mounting systems and accessories
  • No or low copay

What You'll Need:

  • Same documentation as private insurance
  • May require prior authorization
  • Device must be prescribed by approved vendor

Timeline:

Varies widely by state, typically 2-4 months

3. School District

Under IDEA, schools must provide assistive technology (including AAC) if it's required for a student to receive a free appropriate public education (FAPE).

What Schools Might Provide:

  • AAC devices (tablets with apps or dedicated devices)
  • AAC apps and software
  • Low-tech communication boards
  • Mounting systems for use at school
  • Training for staff

Important Notes:

  • The device belongs to the school, not the family
  • Must be included in the IEP
  • Schools may not allow devices to go home
  • Some districts have device "libraries" students can borrow from

School vs. Personal Device

If possible, get a personal device through insurance so your child has access at home, on weekends, and over summer break. School devices often stay at school, limiting your child's communication outside of school hours.

4. Out-of-Pocket Purchase

For AAC apps on consumer tablets, many families choose to purchase directly.

Cost Range:

  • Free apps: CoughDrop (limited features), AAC Speech Buddy
  • Low-cost apps ($10-$100): Various starter AAC apps
  • Mid-range apps ($100-$300): Proloquo2Go ($300), TouchChat ($300), LAMP Words for Life ($300)
  • Tablet: iPad ($300-$1,000 depending on model)

Advantages:

  • Immediate access (no waiting for approval)
  • Full ownership and control
  • Can be used anywhere
  • Can try different apps to see what works

Start Here While Waiting for Insurance

If you can afford it, purchasing an AAC app on a tablet gives your child communication now while you work through the insurance process for a dedicated device. The skills they learn will transfer.

5. Grants and Nonprofit Organizations

Various organizations provide funding assistance for AAC devices.

Organizations That May Help:

  • AAC Funding Help: Lists grants and funding sources by state
  • United Healthcare Children's Foundation: Grants up to $5,000 for medical equipment
  • The Luka Autism Foundation: Provides iPads with AAC apps
  • Variety - The Children's Charity: Provides communication devices
  • Local service organizations: Rotary, Lions Club, Knights of Columbus often fund medical equipment
  • Crowdfunding: GoFundMe, Facebook fundraisers

What to Know:

  • Application processes vary
  • Often requires proof of financial need
  • May take several months
  • Usually one-time assistance

The Insurance Process: Step by Step

  1. Check Your Coverage: Call insurance and ask about SGD coverage. Get details in writing if possible.
  2. Get an AAC Evaluation: Work with an SLP who has AAC expertise. The evaluation report is critical for insurance approval.
  3. Complete a Trial Period: Insurance wants proof the device works for your child. Document everything during trials.
  4. Get a Prescription: Your child's doctor must prescribe the device. Some insurance requires specific specialists (neurologist, developmental pediatrician).
  5. Submit Letter of Medical Necessity: The SLP writes this explaining why your child needs the specific device recommended.
  6. Wait for Approval: This can take weeks to months. Follow up regularly.
  7. If Denied, Appeal: First denials are common. Don't give up. The appeals process often results in approval.
  8. Order the Device: Once approved, the vendor orders and programs the device.
  9. Delivery and Training: The vendor delivers the device and provides initial training.

Writing a Strong Letter of Medical Necessity

The letter of medical necessity is the most important document in your insurance packet. It must clearly explain:

Letter Should Include:

  • Medical diagnosis and how it affects communication
  • Current communication abilities (what your child can and cannot do)
  • Why speech therapy alone is not sufficient
  • Specific device being requested and why
  • Why less expensive options won't work (must document trials of simpler systems)
  • How the device will improve your child's medical condition and quality of life
  • Impact on safety (ability to communicate pain, needs, emergencies)
  • Expected functional improvements (requesting items, answering questions, participating in education)
  • Documentation of successful trial with requested device
  • SLP's credentials and experience with AAC

Medical Necessity, Not Educational

Insurance covers medical needs, not educational ones. Frame everything in terms of health, safety, and medical necessity. Focus on how AAC will help your child communicate medical needs, not how it will help them in school.

If Insurance Denies Your Request

Don't panic. Initial denials are extremely common, and many families succeed on appeal.

Common Denial Reasons:

  • "Not medically necessary" (most common)
  • "Experimental or investigational"
  • "Educational, not medical"
  • "Less costly alternative available"
  • "Insufficient documentation"

How to Appeal:

  1. Request the denial in writing and understand the specific reason
  2. Review your insurance policy language about DME and SGDs
  3. Gather additional documentation addressing the denial reason
  4. Write an appeal letter responding to each denial point
  5. Include research studies showing AAC effectiveness
  6. Get letters of support from doctors, therapists, teachers
  7. Document medical necessity (safety, ability to communicate pain/needs)
  8. Submit the appeal within the deadline (usually 30-60 days)
  9. If denied again, request external review (independent reviewer outside the insurance company)

Don't Miss Appeal Deadlines

Insurance companies have strict deadlines for appeals. Missing a deadline can mean starting over. Track all dates carefully and submit appeals well before deadlines.

Working with AAC Vendors

AAC vendors (companies that sell devices) often help with the insurance process.

What Vendors Can Do:

  • Provide devices for trial periods
  • Submit insurance paperwork on your behalf
  • Follow up with insurance companies
  • Provide documentation needed for approval
  • Program and customize devices
  • Deliver and provide initial training

Choosing a Vendor:

  • Ask your SLP for recommendations
  • Check if they're in-network with your insurance
  • Ask about their experience with your insurance company
  • Find out what support they provide after device delivery

Funding FAQs

Can I get both a school device and a personal device?

Yes! Schools provide devices for educational use, and insurance provides devices for medical use. Your child can have both. This ensures communication access at school and at home.

Will insurance cover an iPad?

Rarely. Insurance typically only covers dedicated SGDs, not consumer tablets. However, some families have had success arguing that an iPad with a specific AAC app is medically necessary when dedicated devices won't work for their child's needs.

How often can I get a new device?

Most insurance companies consider SGDs durable medical equipment with a 5-year lifespan. You can typically request a new device every 5 years, or sooner if the device is broken beyond repair or your child's needs have changed significantly.

What if my child loses or breaks the device?

Warranties typically cover defects, not loss or damage. Some insurance plans cover one replacement per year. Consider getting a protection plan or insurance rider for expensive devices. Always have a low-tech backup system.

Can I get financial assistance if I don't have insurance?

Yes. Look into Medicaid eligibility, grants from nonprofit organizations, and crowdfunding. Also consider starting with a less expensive AAC app while working on funding for a dedicated device.

Bottom Line

Funding AAC devices can be frustrating and time-consuming, but it's worth the effort. Don't let cost prevent your child from getting communication. Multiple funding sources exist, and persistence pays off. Many families who were initially denied eventually got their devices approved.

Remember: Communication is not a luxury. It's a basic human right and a medical necessity. Keep advocating.
