Choosing the Right AAC System

There is no single "best" AAC device. The right choice depends on your child's individual needs, abilities, and communication goals. Most AAC users benefit from having multiple communication options for different situations.

Types of AAC Systems

Low-Tech AAC

No batteries or electronics required. These are simple, portable, and durable options.

Examples:

  • Picture boards
  • Communication books or binders
  • Core word boards
  • Visual schedules
  • Choice boards

Pros

  • Inexpensive or free
  • Always available (no charging needed)
  • Easy to customize
  • Durable
  • No learning curve for partners

Cons

  • Limited vocabulary
  • No voice output
  • Requires a communication partner
  • Can be lost or damaged
  • May be stigmatizing for older users

Best for: Early communicators, backup communication, specific routines or activities, situations where high-tech devices aren't practical

Mid-Tech AAC

Simple electronic devices with voice output, typically with limited vocabulary.

Examples:

  • Single-message devices (BIGmack, Step-by-Step)
  • Simple multi-message devices (GoTalk, QuickTalker)
  • Voice output communication aids (VOCAs)

Pros

  • Voice output increases independence
  • Relatively affordable
  • Durable
  • Easy to program
  • Simple to use

Cons

  • Very limited vocabulary
  • Not designed for complex language
  • Requires frequent reprogramming
  • May not grow with the child

Best for: Specific activities, early switch users, testing whether voice output is motivating, backup devices

High-Tech AAC: Tablets with AAC Apps

Consumer tablets (iPad, Android) with specialized AAC software.

Popular AAC Apps:

  • Proloquo2Go: Symbol-based, customizable, works offline
  • TouchChat: Multiple vocabulary options (WordPower, UNITY), supports symbol and text
  • LAMP Words for Life: Motor planning approach, consistent motor patterns
  • Avaz: Picture-based, includes literacy support
  • CoughDrop: Free/low-cost, cloud-based, highly customizable
  • AssistiveWare Proloquo: Newer app with simplified interface

Pros

  • More affordable than dedicated devices
  • Multi-purpose (AAC + other apps)
  • Socially acceptable (everyone has tablets)
  • Easy to update and customize
  • Large vocabulary capacity
  • Can grow with the user

Cons

  • Distractions from other apps/notifications
  • Less durable than dedicated devices
  • May not be covered by insurance
  • Battery life varies
  • Mounting/positioning can be challenging

Best for: Children learning AAC, families wanting flexibility, users who need multiple communication functions, situations where cost is a barrier

High-Tech AAC: Dedicated Speech-Generating Devices (SGDs)

Purpose-built devices designed specifically for communication.

Popular Dedicated Devices:

  • Tobii Dynavox devices: I-Series, TD Pilot (eye gaze and touch options)
  • PRC devices: Accent, NOVA chat (run Unityand LAMP WFL)
  • Lingraphica: Designed for aphasia/stroke survivors
  • Saltillo: ChatFusion, TouchChat Express (similar to app versions)

Pros

  • Built for communication (no distractions)
  • Extremely durable
  • Often covered by insurance/Medicaid
  • Amplified speakers for noisy environments
  • Mounting solutions included
  • Advanced access methods (eye gaze, switch scanning)

Cons

  • Very expensive ($8,000-$15,000+)
  • May draw unwanted attention
  • Heavy and bulky
  • Insurance process is lengthy
  • Updates can be slow

Best for: Users who need specialized access methods, users in challenging environments (loud, outdoor), users who benefit from having a dedicated communication device, situations where insurance will cover the cost

Key Factors to Consider

Factor Questions to Ask
Motor Skills How does your child access items? Direct touch? Eye gaze? Switch? Head pointer?
Vision Can your child see small symbols? Do they need high contrast? Large icons?
Hearing Does your child rely on auditory feedback? Do they need amplified volume?
Cognitive/Language Skills Does your child understand pictures? Words? How many choices can they handle at once?
Communication Goals What does your child need to communicate about? Needs? Wants? Stories? Academic content?
Environment Where will the device be used? Home? School? Community? Outdoors?
Portability Does your child need to carry it? Will it be mounted on a wheelchair?
Durability Will the device be dropped? Exposed to liquids? Used roughly?
Cost What can you afford? Will insurance cover it? Can the school provide it?
Partner Training How much training will family/staff need? How complex is the system?

Vocabulary Organization Systems

Beyond the device itself, how vocabulary is organized matters. Common systems include:

Core Vocabulary

Focuses on high-frequency words (go, want, like, more, stop) that can be combined to say many things. Core words make up 80% of what we say.

Examples: TouchChat with WordPower, Proloquo2Go Crescendo

Semantic-Syntactic (Category-Based)

Organizes words by category (people, places, actions, descriptions). User navigates through categories to find words.

Examples: Proloquo2Go, Gateway (in TouchChat)

Motor Planning (LAMP, Unity)

Icons are always in the same location. User learns consistent motor patterns to access words, similar to typing.

Examples: LAMP Words for Life, Unity in TouchChat, PRC Accent with Unity

There's No Wrong Choice

The "best" system is the one your child will actually use. Many AAC users try several systems before finding what works. Skills learned on one system transfer to others. Starting with something is better than waiting for the perfect option.

Common Questions

Should I start with low-tech or high-tech?

You don't have to choose one or the other. Many families use both. Low-tech options can be used right away while you're working on getting a high-tech device. Even after getting a high-tech device, low-tech options are useful backups.

What if we choose the wrong device?

There's no "wrong" device. Your child will learn communication skills that transfer across systems. If a device isn't working, you can try something else. Trial periods exist for exactly this reason.

Should I get an iPad or a dedicated device?

Both have pros and cons. iPads are cheaper, more socially acceptable, and multi-purpose. Dedicated devices are more durable, have better speakers, and may be covered by insurance. Many families start with an iPad and transition to a dedicated device later if needed.

How many words should the device have?

Your child's device should have room to grow. Even if they're only using 10-20 words now, the system should be able to expand to hundreds or thousands of words as their skills develop. Don't limit their future potential with a device that's too simple.

What if my child doesn't like the device we chose?

Resistance is common at first. Give it time. Make sure adults are modeling use of the device. If after several months your child still isn't using it, reassess with your SLP. The issue might be how it's being taught, not the device itself.
