What is AAC?

Augmentative and Alternative Communication (AAC) includes all forms of communication that supplement or replace spoken language. This can range from simple picture boards to sophisticated speech-generating devices.

Augmentative means it adds to or supports existing speech. Alternative means it replaces speech entirely when speaking is not possible.

Who Uses AAC?

AAC is used by people of all ages who have difficulty speaking or being understood. This includes people with:

  • Autism spectrum disorder
  • Cerebral palsy
  • Intellectual disabilities
  • Apraxia of speech
  • Stroke or traumatic brain injury
  • ALS or other degenerative conditions
  • Temporary conditions (after surgery, intubation, etc.)

Types of AAC

Unaided AAC

Communication that doesn't require any external tools:

  • Gestures and body language
  • Facial expressions
  • Sign language
  • Vocalizations

Aided AAC

Communication that uses external tools or devices:

  • Low-tech: Picture boards, communication books, pointing to letters
  • Mid-tech: Single-message voice output devices, simple communication boards with voice output
  • High-tech: Speech-generating devices (SGDs), tablets with AAC apps, eye-gaze systems

Common Myths About AAC

Myth: AAC will prevent my child from learning to talk

Truth: Research shows AAC actually supports speech development

Using AAC gives children a way to communicate while they're developing speech. Studies consistently show that AAC does not hinder speech development and often helps it. Many children who use AAC also develop some spoken words over time.

Myth: My child needs to try everything else first before AAC

Truth: Waiting to introduce AAC denies your child communication

There are no prerequisites for AAC. Children don't need to "fail" at speech therapy first. Early access to AAC gives children a way to communicate their needs, participate in learning, and develop language skills while other interventions continue.

Myth: AAC is only for people who will never speak

Truth: AAC supports communication in many situations

Some people use AAC all the time, while others use it only in certain situations (when tired, stressed, or in loud environments). AAC is a tool that can be used as much or as little as needed.

Myth: My child isn't smart enough to use AAC

Truth: AAC can be adapted for any skill level

AAC systems range from very simple (one or two pictures) to very complex (thousands of words). The system grows with your child. Every person deserves a way to communicate, regardless of cognitive ability.

Myth: AAC is too expensive

Truth: There are AAC options at every price point

While dedicated speech-generating devices can be expensive, many are covered by insurance or school districts. There are also free and low-cost AAC apps available. Don't let cost prevent you from exploring AAC for your child.

Why AAC Matters

Communication is a basic human right. When children can't communicate their needs, wants, thoughts, and feelings, it affects every part of their life:

  • Learning: Children need to communicate to participate in education
  • Relationships: Communication builds connections with family and friends
  • Safety: Being able to communicate pain, fear, or emergencies is critical
  • Independence: Communication gives people control over their own lives
  • Mental health: Frustration from not being understood can lead to anxiety and behavior challenges

AAC gives children a voice. It opens doors to learning, relationships, and independence that might otherwise be closed.
